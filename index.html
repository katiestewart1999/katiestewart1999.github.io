Types of Volcano

When magma erupts at the surface it can form different types of volcanoes depending on the
viscosity, or stickiness, of the magma, the amount of gas in the magma, and the way in which
the magma reached the surface. The types of volcano include cinder cones, composite volcanoes,
lava domes and shield volcanoes.

Composite volcanoes
These are usually the largest volcanoes with steep sides and a symmetrical cone
shape. They form very thick, viscous and sticky lava that does not flow easily.
The eruptions are very explosive as the gas can't leave the very thick magma and
the pressure then builds up.

Lava Dome Volcanoes
These are usually very rounded and have more gentle slopes. They are formed by the
slow extrusion of viscous lava from a volcano. These eruptions are common,
particularly at a convergent plate boundary. Lava domes are unpredictable due to
non-linear dynamics.

Cinder Cone Volcanoes
These are built from particles and blocks of congealated lava ejected from a
single vent. As the gas chanrged lava is blown violently into the air, it breaks
into small fragments that solidify and fall as cinders around the vent to form
a circular or oval cone.

Shield Volcanoes
These are formed when a volcano produces low viscosity, runny lava that spreads
far from the source. This forms a volcano with gentle slopes. Shield volcanoes tend
to be the largest in terms of area as they erupt regularly and for long periods of
time. This results in lots of layers building up.
